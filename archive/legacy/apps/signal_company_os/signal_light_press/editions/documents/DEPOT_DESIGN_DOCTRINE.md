I. GOVERNING PRINCIPLE



Movement fails first.

Storage endures.



A Signal Depot exists to outlast interruption, not to maximize throughput.



II. DEFINITION — SIGNAL DEPOT



A Signal Depot is a bounded, place-anchored infrastructure node that provides:



Temporal buffering



Thermal or material preservation



Decision pause



Resource grounding



Cultural and architectural continuity



A Depot allows an Arc to stop without breaking.



III. WHAT A DEPOT IS NOT



A Signal Depot is not:



A distribution center



A fulfillment warehouse



A logistics hub optimized for speed



A speculative real-estate asset



A disposable utility structure



Any node optimized primarily for velocity, turnover, or scale is non-canonical.



IV. CORE FUNCTIONS (NON-NEGOTIABLE)



Every canonical Depot must provide all of the following, regardless of size or technology.



1\. Holding Function



The ability to retain goods, energy, or materials without immediate onward movement.



Holding may be:



Cold



Dry



Passive



Seasonal



Emergency-rated



But it must exist.



2\. Deceleration Function



A Depot must slow the system.



This includes:



Intake pacing



Dispatch restraint



Human review



Posture reassessment



If a node accelerates decisions, it is not a Depot.



3\. Stabilization Function



A Depot absorbs shocks including:



Weather interruption



Transport failure



Market volatility



Human capacity limits



The Arc stabilizes around the Depot.



4\. Autonomy Function



A Depot must operate independently for a bounded period.



This includes:



Energy resilience



Thermal persistence



Security



Local decision authority



A node that cannot stand alone is not a Depot.

Active — receiving, holding, dispatching



Holding — receiving only, no dispatch



Dormant — sealed, maintained, watchful



Emergency — drawdown / surge mode



Recovery — restocking, recalibration

VI. DEPOT CLASSIFICATIONS (ABSTRACT)



These are types, not buildings.



Type I — Cold Depot



Type II — Dry / Durable Depot



Type III — Hybrid Depot



Type IV — Emergency Depot



Type V — Cultural / Guild Depot



A single physical structure may fulfill multiple types.



VII. SCALE INVARIANCE RULE



A Depot’s validity is independent of size.



A historic icehouse, timber-frame barn, modern cold shed, or stone root cellar may all be canonical Depots if they fulfill the required functions.



Conversely, a million-square-foot warehouse may fail to qualify.



VIII. DEPOT–ARC RELATIONSHIP



An Arc may not exist without at least one Depot



A Depot may exist without an active Arc



Depots anchor Arcs; Arcs do not define Depots



This asymmetry is intentional.



IX. DEPOT–WARDEN RELATIONSHIP



Depots fall under Sea, Hearth, or Craft Wardens depending on function



No Depot may self-optimize outside Engine governance



Depots may be shared across Domains only with doctrine approval



X. ARCHITECTURAL LINEAGE \& REGIONAL STEWARDSHIP

X.1 Governing Principle



A Signal Depot must belong to its land before it serves its system.



Depots are civic and cultural artifacts, not neutral infrastructure.



X.2 New England Icehouse Lineage (Canonical)



All Signal Depots in New England are canonically derived from the historic New England icehouse and its associated agricultural outbuildings.



Historic icehouses embodied the exact principles required of a Signal Depot:



Passive thermal persistence



Thick envelopes and slow exchange



Seasonal operation and dormancy



Mass, gravity, and insulation over machinery



Integration with barns, yards, and working homesteads



Longevity measured in generations



The icehouse is the ancestral cold chain.



Signal Depots are its modern continuation.



X.3 Architectural Continuity Rule



A Signal Depot must visually, materially, and structurally align with regional New England working architecture, including:



Icehouses



Timber-frame barns



Carriage sheds



Sugar houses



Root cellars



Wood sheds



Shore sheds and fish houses



This continuity is functional inheritance, not aesthetic nostalgia.



X.4 Adaptive Reuse \& Preservation Priority



Wherever possible, Signal Depots shall be instantiated through:



Adaptive reuse of existing structures



Preservation-first renovation



Reactivation of underutilized barns and homesteads



Integration into working landscapes



Demolition-first or greenfield construction is disfavored unless no viable alternative exists.



X.5 Material \& Form Doctrine



Canonical depots favor:



Timber framing or heavy wood construction



Thick wall assemblies (mass + insulation)



Lime-based finishes where appropriate



Passive ventilation and drainage



Minimal reliance on complex mechanical systems



Repairability over optimization



The goal is graceful aging, not technical perfection.



X.6 Cultural Stewardship Function



A Signal Depot is also a cultural stabilizer.



Each Depot should:



Preserve local building knowledge



Maintain continuity of rural working forms



Avoid visual disruption of historic landscapes



Signal care, restraint, and permanence



A Depot that alienates its community has already failed.



X.7 Canonical Constraint



No Signal Depot may be designed in a form that could not plausibly have existed in New England 150 years ago — even if its function is modern.



Modern systems may be embedded.

Modern forms are not required.



XI. FINAL CANONICAL RULE



A Depot is a promise to hold when movement becomes irresponsible.
